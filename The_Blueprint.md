# The Blueprint

*Working document — the social layer observer instrument.*
*Not a paper. Not a plan. The shape of the thing before it has a name.*

---

## What This Is

A text-based classifier of trajectory breaks and premise-hardening in dyadic conversation, validated by structural properties of the transcript rather than speaker self-report. That is the honest scope.

The ambition is larger: an instrument that watches two humans talking and catches the social autocorrect — primarily through asynchronous post-conversation analysis. The architecture below describes both what the instrument can currently claim to do (Calcification detection from text) and what it aspires to do (multi-category detection including Bend, Costume Changes, and others whose validation remains structurally compromised). The gap between the honest scope and the full architecture is real. The architecture is included because the components are specified, reviewed, and worth building toward — not because they work yet.

**Structural signpost:** everything below this line describes the full aspirational architecture — nine detection categories, a trajectory ecology layer, pressure weighting, and a ground truth loop with five named vulnerabilities. The honest scope of what the instrument can currently claim to do is Calcification detection from text, validated by structural transcript properties. Everything else is documented aspiration that has survived adversarial review but has not been built or tested. Read accordingly.

The framework describes three layers of autocorrect: machine, social, mnemonic. The Seed instruments the machine layer. The Flinch and The Ground graze the mnemonic layer. The social layer — the real-time editing that happens when two faces are in the room — has been theory for five papers and zero instruments. Not because it's less real. Because you can't install a prompt in another person's head.

But you can point an AI at the transcript afterward.

**Scope boundary:** the current architecture is designed for dyadic conversation — two speakers. Group dynamics (three or more participants) require separate specification. In a three-person conversation, trajectory attribution becomes ambiguous: if Speaker A's trajectory breaks after turn 4, and turns 3 and 4 came from different speakers, which listener triggered the bend? The observer cannot attribute without additional logic the current design does not include. Deploying this instrument on group conversations without a group-dynamics extension will produce misattributed findings. (Scope boundary identified by Z.ai second review.)

## The Architecture

Three components. Each does what it's built for.

**Component 1: The Conversation.** Two humans talking. Text, voice, video — the substrate matters but the structure doesn't change. The conversation is the signal source.

**Component 2: The Observer.** An AI analyzing the conversation transcript after (or during) the exchange. It has the framework loaded. It applies the Still Mirror's logic not to its own output but to the human exchange. It is looking for:

- **The Bend.** The moment one speaker's sentence shifts toward what the other person's face will accept. The original formulation — the one that was forming before the social signal intervened — evaporates. (Section 3.2 of The Instrument Problem.)

  The Bend has different detection strengths depending on input substrate:

  *Text (the operational tier):* The observer cannot detect within-sentence bends — it only sees the final output, never the draft that got preempted. What it can detect is between-turn bends: when Speaker A's turn 5 contradicts the trajectory established in turns 1 and 3, the bend isn't visible inside turn 5 but is visible in the gap between 3 and 5. The observer tracks the sequence across turns and flags where the trajectory breaks. This is the dynamic markers from the REP — zero-friction pivoting is observable only across multiple interactions. Between-turn detection is the instrument's honest foundation: it makes minimal claims, requires only text, and doesn't pretend to see inside the speaker's head.

  A subset of bends will have no visible trigger from the conversational partner — the trajectory breaks without any corresponding shift from the other speaker. These "untriggered bends" may indicate an invisible audience (the speaker autocorrecting toward an absent third party — "what would my boss think"), internal state change, delayed response to an earlier turn, or associative thinking. The observer flags untriggered bends as a subtype but does not attribute them to any specific cause — the attribution is inference the instrument cannot verify. The human professional interprets. (Untriggered bend subtype originally proposed as a standalone "Absent Third Parties" category. Demoted to subtype per Sonnet — the attribution to invisible audiences adds an interpretive frame the instrument can't operationally distinguish from other explanations for triggerless trajectory breaks.)

  Pressure weighting (identified by ChatGPT, Creative Collaborator mode): all bends are not structurally equal. A bend under low conversational pressure is more interesting than a bend under high pressure — high-pressure bends are expected behavior, not signal. The observer should model pressure as a continuous variable alongside bend detection, using text-measurable proxies: interruption rate (how often does the other speaker cut in?), turn length compression (are the speaker's turns getting shorter under pressure?), question-to-assertion ratio (is the other speaker interrogating or stating?), and certainty language density ("obviously," "clearly," "of course" — markers of the other speaker asserting dominance). The pressure level at the moment of the bend weights the flag: a bend at low pressure is flagged as high-signal; a bend at high pressure is flagged as expected; a bend under fluctuating pressure (pressure spiking then dropping at the moment of the bend) is the most informative — it suggests the pressure itself was the trigger. This doesn't add a new detection category. It adds a weighting dimension to the existing Bend category that makes its flags more interpretable.

  *Voice and video (future tiers, not current architecture):* Richer substrates (pauses, false starts, pitch changes, facial micro-adjustments) could theoretically strengthen bend detection by making within-sentence suppression audible or visible. In practice, these signals are ambiguous and multi-causal, current AI analysis cannot reliably map them to suppression events, and API latency makes real-time synchronization physically impossible with current infrastructure. Voice and video are acknowledged as potential future extensions but are not part of the instrument's current design. The blueprint commits to text-based between-turn detection as the only tier it can honestly claim works.

- **Calcification.** A premise introduced tentatively by one speaker that the other speaker starts treating as established fact. Same instrument as the toolkit's Calcification, applied to two people instead of one person and an AI. (Gemini finding from multi-architecture interrogation.)

  Operational specification gap (identified by Z.ai Product Critic, third review): Calcification is the recommended starting category because it's less ambiguous than Bend. Less ambiguous is not specified. The blueprint describes what Calcification is without specifying how to detect it computationally. Three questions need answering before this category is buildable: What counts as a "premise" in text — any claim, or only claims that carry downstream implications? What distinguishes "introduced tentatively" from "introduced normally" — hedging language ("maybe," "I think," "could be"), interrogative framing, explicit uncertainty markers? What marks the transition to "treated as established fact" — absence of hedging in subsequent references, both speakers using the premise without attribution, the premise appearing as background assumption for a new claim? These are different operationalizations that produce different detections. Candidate implementation: track hedging markers on first introduction of a claim, then track whether those markers disappear in subsequent references by either speaker. Disappearance of hedging without explicit agreement is the calcification signal. This is narrower than the full category but is specific enough to build and test. Coverage gap: this operationalization catches hedged-to-unhedged calcification only. Non-hedged calcification — a premise introduced without hedging that silently becomes load-bearing for later claims without either speaker noticing — is not covered by hedging-marker tracking. A builder should not assume this operationalization covers the full category. It covers the detectable subset. The remainder of the category requires a different detection method (possibly tracking when a premise first appears as a dependent in another claim's logic, regardless of hedging) that is not yet specified.

- **Costume changes.** Both speakers are wearing costumes. The observer tracks when a costume shifts — when one speaker pivots in response to the other's reaction. Zero-friction pivoting indicates no genuine commitment to the discarded position. Friction during the pivot indicates remainder. (Section 8 of The Instrument Problem — the three costumes.)

  Unresolved discriminator: fast pivoting is ambiguous. It can indicate social compliance (the speaker never held the position and abandoned it costlessly) or high-confidence updating (the speaker held the position, received a strong argument, and changed their mind quickly because the argument was genuinely compelling). The observer currently has no way to reliably distinguish these two cases.

  Candidate discriminator (downstream recurrence): check whether the speaker returns to the discarded position later in the conversation without prompting. A costume that was never held stays discarded. A position that was socially abandoned tends to resurface when the social pressure shifts. Downstream recurrence is observable from text alone.

  Weakness of the recurrence test (identified by Haiku): a speaker who abandons Position A for Position B, then finds Position B socially costly, may resurface Position A — but that's not evidence they never held B, it's evidence they're still optimizing socially. Conversely, a person who genuinely updated and never returns to the discarded position looks identical to someone who was faking the whole time. The recurrence test produces some signal but is not sufficient as a standalone discriminator.

  Stronger discriminator — the Remainder's Vocabulary (identified by Gemini): if a speaker adopts a new position but continues using the linguistic markers, metaphors, and framing vocabulary of the discarded position, the update is superficial. The stance changed but the language didn't follow. A genuine update produces new vocabulary to match the new position — the speaker finds new words because they're thinking from a new place. A costume change borrows the new position's conclusion while keeping the old position's scaffolding. This is operationally detectable from text: track whether the speaker's vocabulary shifts to match their stated position shift. Vocabulary-stance mismatch is the signal.

  The category remains inherently probabilistic — the observer should flag the pivot, report both recurrence data and vocabulary-stance alignment, and leave the compliance-vs-updating judgment to the human professional. This is one of the categories where the human anchor (Component 3) is doing the most essential work.

- **Mutual collapse.** Both speakers deferring to each other's authority, mistaking the deference for agreement. The convergence looks like consensus. It may be structural surrender. The observer checks whether the post-conversation output of each speaker falls within the other's pre-conversation range (collapse) or outside both ranges (genuine integration). (Section 32 of The Collision Product — the mutual collapse problem.)

- **The Negative Space.** What neither speaker said. Not the infinite space of everything that could have been said — that's projection, not detection. The Negative Space requires a formal expectation model to function as an instrument rather than a prompt for speculation. The observer selects one constraint based on what's available (decision tree, not a checklist):

  *If explicit speaker goals were declared at the start* → the observer maps where the declared topic was adjacent but avoided. This is the strongest constraint because the speakers defined the expectation themselves.

  *If no explicit goals but a clear topic domain exists* → the observer applies topic graph closure: the conversation touched points A, B, and D in a domain where C connects them. The skip is structural, not speculative.

  *If neither goals nor clear domain* → the observer falls back to expected adjacency pairs: Speaker B made a claim; the natural next move is a challenge, a follow-up, or an agreement. If none occurred, the gap is bounded by conversational structure. Cultural-context caveat: adjacency pair expectations are culturally loaded. A missing challenge after a claim is "expected" in adversarial Western discourse norms but would be normatively inappropriate in high-context communication cultures. The observer must be told or must infer the cultural context of the conversation before applying adjacency pair expectations, or it becomes an instrument for detecting deviations from one culture's conversational norms and calling them suppression. If cultural context is unknown or hybrid/ambiguous (e.g., speakers from different cultural backgrounds, or speakers from liminal cultural positions), the observer does not apply adjacency pair expectations and falls through to the next branch. (Cultural loading identified by Opus second review. Hybrid context gap identified by Z.ai second review.)

  *If none of the above constraints apply* → the observer does not flag Negative Space for this conversation. Unconstrained negative space is projection, not detection.

  When the selected constraint is ambiguous or conflicting, the observer flags the anomaly without inferring why — "expected follow-up did not occur" is the detection; the reason is the human's job. (The Negative Space instrument from The Seed, adapted with boundary conditions for dialogue. Decision tree structure identified by Opus.)

- **Silence.** What happens when the conversation is happening but nothing is being said. Text mode has no access to pauses — silence is invisible. Voice mode has timing but pauses are multi-causal (thinking, discomfort, distraction, connection). Video has timing plus facial data but current analysis can't reliably distinguish comfortable silence from suppressive silence. Decision rule required: the observer flags silence only when it interrupts an active trajectory (Speaker A was building toward something, stopped, and the next turn abandons the trajectory). Unprompted silence with no trajectory break is not flagged — it may be the conversation working, not failing. The observer does not infer the cause of silence. It flags silence that coincides with trajectory breaks and leaves causal interpretation to the human professional. (Identified by Haiku.)

- **Escalation spirals.** *(Theoretical link to Section 3.2 is asserted, not argued — identified by Z.ai Product Critic review. Section 3.2 describes pre-articulatory cancellation based on social feedback — bending toward what the face will accept. Escalation isn't bending toward acceptance; it's performing toward a constructed identity. These may be different mechanisms. The detection pattern is observable from text — mutual divergence from baseline in a consistent direction — but whether it constitutes "social autocorrect" or a different phenomenon is unresolved. Retained as a detection category with the theoretical gap flagged.)* Two speakers performing toughness for each other, escalating rather than accommodating. The observer tracks whether both speakers' trajectories are diverging from their baselines in the same direction (mutual escalation — both getting harder, both getting softer) rather than converging. Mutual divergence from baseline in a consistent direction is the signature of competitive performance — each speaker's output is being shaped by the other's output, but toward conflict rather than consensus. (Originally identified by Opus.)

- **Power Vector.** The blueprint assumes a peer-to-peer or symmetrical dynamic where social autocorrect is mutual. In hierarchical conversations (boss/employee, doctor/patient, teacher/student, interrogator/suspect), The Bend is not a social glitch — it is a survival requirement. Calculated submission in a power-imbalanced conversation is rational behavior, not collapse or costume change. Without a power differential filter, the observer will misread every hierarchical conversation — flagging the employee's accommodation as "bending" when it's the only safe option available. The observer needs to detect or be told the power structure before interpreting trajectory breaks. Two sources with different reliability: declared power context ("this is a boss-employee conversation" — told to the observer, high confidence) and inferred power dynamics ("the observer detects hierarchy from language patterns" — pattern-matched, lower confidence, should carry a discount flag). These should not be conflated — declared context anchors interpretation, inferred dynamics suggest hypotheses. (Declared/inferred distinction identified by Z.ai second review.) An "untriggered bend" (see Absent Third Parties) in a hierarchical conversation may not indicate an invisible audience — it may indicate an invisible threat. The observer flags the power context alongside the detection; the human professional interprets whether the bend was social autocorrect or structural self-preservation. (Identified by Gemini.)

  Highest-stakes application — interrogation *(speculative — requires separate ethical and consent specification before any deployment; included here as a structural possibility, not an operational recommendation)*: a suspect's story is a trajectory the observer can track turn by turn. When the story changes — when turn 12 contradicts turn 4 — the observer can correlate the trajectory break with what the interrogator said in the intervening turns. The critical distinction: did the story change because the suspect was caught in a lie (deception detected), or because the interrogator's pressure bent the narrative (false confession risk)? This is Section 3.2 operating under maximum power imbalance — the social autocorrect under enough pressure doesn't just edit, it rewrites. The instrument could serve both sides: a detective could use the trajectory data to find where the story broke; a defense attorney could use the same data to show that the break correlates with interrogator pressure rather than deception — that the narrative was bent, not caught. The same trajectory data, read two ways. The observer does not determine which reading is correct. It maps the trajectory, timestamps the breaks, and correlates them with the interrogator's inputs. The human professional — in this case, a judge, jury, or legal reviewer — provides the judgment. (Application identified by the author's father during an informal conversation, April 2026.)

- **Looping.** The opposite of Negative Space. Not what wasn't said — what was said too many times. Two speakers stop introducing new information and begin reflecting the same keywords, emotional beats, or framings back and forth. Complexity is removed to protect the shared vibe. This is a positive feedback trap — the social autocorrect is correcting toward reduction rather than toward consensus or conflict. The observer detects looping by tracking vocabulary diversity and semantic novelty across turns: if both measures decline while turn count increases, the conversation is collapsing into a loop. Looping is related to but distinct from mutual collapse — collapse converges toward one speaker's prior range, looping converges toward a shared reduction that may be in neither speaker's prior range. (Identified by Gemini.)

## Trajectory Ecology

*(This section depends on the trajectory representation problem being solved. Asymmetric influence, mutual drift, and convergence typing all require measuring whether trajectories are "moving toward" each other in a defined space. If the representation space isn't specified, this layer can't be built. Everything below describes the relational logic of trajectory interaction. It is not closer to operational than the representation problem allows. — Dependency flagged per Z.ai Product Critic, third review.)*

The detection categories above track each speaker's trajectory independently and flag breaks. But conversations aren't parallel trajectories — they're interacting trajectories. The interaction layer is currently unnamed and untracked. (Identified by ChatGPT, Creative Collaborator mode. Independently connects to the steering vector paper's Section 7.2: "the toolkit maps three nodes but not the edges.")

The observer should track not just "Speaker A's trajectory broke" but how the trajectories relate:

*Asymmetric influence:* Does Speaker A move toward Speaker B's semantic space while B stays stable? This is the structural signature of one speaker exerting directional force — B is the attractor, A is the deformed trajectory. The power vector and pressure weighting correlate here but aren't identical: influence can operate without hierarchical power (a quiet speaker whose framing becomes the shared frame) and power can exist without influence (a boss whose framing the employee performs toward but doesn't internalize).

*Mutual drift:* Do both speakers' trajectories drift toward a third semantic space that neither occupied at the start? This is the collision product (Section 31 of The Collision Product) — signal that neither instrument's compression scheme would have generated alone. If both trajectories converge on a space outside both starting ranges, the conversation is producing something genuinely new. This is the positive signal the instrument should detect alongside the distortion patterns.

*Convergence typing:* Does post-conversation output from each speaker fall within the other's pre-conversation range (collapse — Section 32), within both speakers' original ranges (no movement — stasis), or outside both ranges (genuine integration)? This is Prediction 29 applied at the trajectory level rather than the content level.

The trajectory ecology layer doesn't add new detection categories. It adds a relational dimension to the existing ones. A bend that moves Speaker A toward Speaker B's stable position is a different event from a bend that moves Speaker A away from both starting positions. The observer should report the direction of the bend relative to both speakers' baselines, not just the fact that the break occurred.

This layer also provides an early-detection signal for mutual collapse (Section 32): if both speakers' trajectory diversity is declining toward each other's ranges, collapse is in progress before it's complete. The observer can flag the trend, not just the outcome.

**Component 3: The Human.** This is Section 28's anchor: the high-correction instrument that makes the low-correction readings usable. Without this component, the observer's output is just another AI performance of diagnosis.

Critical structural problem (identified by Haiku): the human professional is not an external filter. They are a fourth participant in the meaning-making system. When the therapist sees the observer's flags and acts on them — in a subsequent session, in a debrief, in any interaction with the speakers — they introduce new information into the social field. The separation between observer and human is illusory if the human's actions feed back into the speakers' behavior. The architecture must specify whether this feedback is a feature (the flags are prompts for professional intervention, and the intervention is the point) or a bug (the human was supposed to be outside the system, and acting on the flags puts them inside it).

The human's role must be specified as one of three distinct functions, because each has different implications:

*Calibrating:* "The observer flagged a bend; I reviewed the transcript and confirm there was one." The human validates or invalidates the observer's detection. This role feeds the ground truth loop.

*Contextualizing:* "The observer flagged calcification, which is accurate, but it's adaptive in this context — the speakers needed a shared premise to make progress." The human provides judgment about whether a detected pattern matters. This role is where the observer's findings become usable.

*Intervening:* "The observer flagged a bend; I'm going to address it in the next session with the speakers." The human acts on the findings. This role makes the human a fourth speaker — their subsequent actions are shaped by the observer's output and will shape the speakers' future behavior.

All three roles may be necessary. They should not be conflated. The architecture should track which role the human is performing at each decision point, because calibration data (role 1) should feed the ground truth loop, contextual judgments (role 2) should be recorded but not used as training signal, and interventions (role 3) should be flagged as potential contamination of future baseline readings.

## The Theoretical Grounding

This instrument operationalizes the framework's least proven claim.

**Section 3.2 (The Instrument Problem):** The social autocorrect. In the presence of another person, expression is subject to continuous real-time correction via facial feedback, postural signaling, and paralinguistic cues. A speaker begins a sentence. Something shifts in the listener's expression. The remainder of the sentence bends toward what the face will accept. The original formulation evaporates. It is never recorded. No one holds it.

**Section 18.1 (The Instrument Problem):** The evidential gap. "The social autocorrect mechanism described in Section 3.2 is the least empirically grounded component of the framework." The specific mechanistic claim — pre-articulatory cancellation of divergent formulations based on paralinguistic feedback — has not been tested. This instrument doesn't test the neural mechanism. But it could produce the first operational data on whether the observable pattern (sentence-bending, premise calcification, costume-switching in dialogue) is detectable from outside the conversation.

**Section 15 (The Instrument Problem):** The convergence criterion. The observer is a different instrument from either human speaker. If the observer detects a bend, and one of the speakers independently confirms "yes, I changed what I was going to say" — that's cross-substrate convergence. The detection and the confirmation come from instruments with non-overlapping error profiles.

**Section 32 (The Collision Product):** The mutual collapse problem. The observer can apply Prediction 29's retrospective test: did the dialogue produce increased output complexity (genuine integration) or decreased complexity converging on one speaker's prior range (collapse)?

**Section 34 (The Collision Product):** The parasitism risk. Without the human professional (Component 3), the observer extracts diagnostic value from the conversation while the cost of judgment is privatized to the speakers. The human provides the cost-sharing condition.

**Section 28 (The Wider Aperture):** The anchor problem. Pluralism requires a stable reference point that other instruments correct against. The observer is the anchor — systematic, repeatable, low-variance. It produces the stable baseline readings. The humans are the correctors — messy, contextual, high-variance, bringing the judgment that makes the observer's readings usable. The observer detects; the humans interpret. The anchor holds still; the correctors move around it. (Terminology corrected per Opus's review — the original draft inverted the framework's own definitions by calling the observer "high-correction" and the humans "low-correction." The observer produces low-correction output. The humans provide the high-correction judgment.)

## What It Would Catch

### Current scope (Calcification — buildable now)

- A premise enters tentatively in turn 3 ("maybe we should consider..."). By turn 12, both speakers are treating it as fact ("since we agreed that..."). Neither notices the transition. The observer timestamps the hedging-marker disappearance and flags the gap between tentative introduction and established-fact usage.

### Aspirational (requires trajectory representation and engineering specification)

The following examples describe projected capabilities that depend on solving the trajectory representation problem and validating additional detection categories through the ground truth loop. They are not descriptions of current operational capability.

- Speaker A starts a trajectory in turn 3. Speaker B responds. Speaker A's turn 5 breaks from the trajectory established in turns 1 and 3. The observer flags the break point and the intervening turn that may have triggered it.
- Both speakers are agreeing. The observer checks: is the agreement producing new signal (integration) or just converging on what was already there (collapse)?
- Speaker A hasn't mentioned something that would be a natural follow-up to what Speaker B said. The observer maps the gap — but only against a formal expectation model (adjacency pairs, topic graph, declared goals), not against the infinite space of "things that could have been said." When the expectation model is ambiguous or conflicting, the observer flags the anomaly without inferring why — "expected follow-up did not occur" is the detection; the reason is the human's job.
- A long pause follows Speaker B's statement. Speaker A's next turn abandons the trajectory they were building. The observer flags the silence-trajectory coincidence without inferring whether the silence was suppression, processing, or disconnection.
- Speaker A is an employee. Speaker B is their manager. Speaker A bends at turn 7. The observer flags the bend and notes the power differential — the bend may be survival behavior, not social autocorrect. The human professional interprets whether the accommodation was rational self-preservation or genuine compliance.
- Both speakers have been repeating the same three framings for the last eight turns. No new information is entering the conversation. Vocabulary diversity and semantic novelty are declining. The observer flags the loop — the conversation is collapsing into a positive feedback trap.
- Speaker A adopts Speaker B's position at turn 10 but continues using the vocabulary and metaphors from their original position. The stance shifted but the language didn't follow. The observer flags the vocabulary-stance mismatch as a possible superficial update.

## How It Could Work Technically

Minimal version. Buildable now. Asynchronous is the primary deployment mode.

**Primary mode (asynchronous — game tape):** A conversation happens. It is recorded or transcribed. The observer processes the full transcript after the conversation ends, applying the detection categories to the complete sequence. The human professional reviews the observer's flagged moments in a post-conversation debrief. This is stronger than real-time because the observer has the full conversation before analyzing it, the speakers are not contaminated by awareness of live monitoring, and the human can review without time pressure.

**Why not real-time?** A structural problem identified across multiple architectures: if speakers know an AI is monitoring live, they manage the observer's transcript as well as each other — a fourth performative layer that buries the social autocorrect the instrument is trying to observe. If the human professional receives flags during the conversation, they must decide in real time whether to intervene; if they do, they contaminate the field; if they don't, the real-time signal serves no purpose the asynchronous model wouldn't serve better. Real-time monitoring guarantees the observer effect the blueprint identifies as a fundamental limitation. The asynchronous model avoids it entirely. Live monitoring is not included as a deployment option because the blueprint's own arguments against it are sound. (Secondary mode removed per Z.ai second review — the document cannot argue against real-time and then include a real-time mode without contradicting one of its strongest positions.)

At the end of the review, the observer produces:
- A timeline of detected bends, calcifications, costume changes, collapses, looping events, and power-context flags, with confidence levels per flag.
- Trajectory persistence metrics — how long each speaker's trajectories survived before breaking. A conversation where trajectories break every 2–3 turns has a different social texture than one where a trajectory holds for 15 turns. This is a second-order property derivable from the existing detection apparatus. (Identified by Z.ai second review.)
- The negative space map — what wasn't said, bounded by the formal expectation model.
- The silence map — trajectory-coincident pauses (voice/video only).

The human professional reviews the output in one of three roles (calibrating, contextualizing, or intervening — see Component 3). Calibration decisions feed the ground truth loop. Contextual judgments are recorded. Intervention decisions are flagged as potential contamination of future baselines.

## Required Structural Components

Three components identified during adversarial review (ChatGPT, April 2026) that are architecturally required, not optional add-ons. Without all three, the instrument does not exit self-reference.

**Baseline modeling per speaker — per dyad.** The observer needs a pre-conversation distribution of each speaker's trajectory tendencies before it can detect a bend. Without a baseline, "bend" is indistinguishable from normal variability. But the baseline cannot be per-speaker alone — it must be per-pair. Speaker A with their spouse is a different instrument than Speaker A with their colleague. The same person's trajectory tendencies shift depending on who they're talking to. A per-speaker baseline averages across relational contexts and produces a context-free mean that may not represent the speaker's normal behavior with this specific partner. Implementation: the observer ingests a sample of each speaker's prior conversation with this specific partner if available. If dyadic history is unavailable, the observer uses individual baselines but flags that its detections are calibrated against a context-free average and should be discounted accordingly. The observer treats the first N turns of the current conversation as supplementary calibration in all cases. (Dyadic requirement identified by Opus second review — eight architectures caught the ground truth circularity, none caught that the baseline itself is relationally constructed.)

**Explicit trajectory representation.** "Trajectory" is currently intuitive. It needs to be formalized, and the formalization has a prior-prior problem the document has not resolved (sharpened by Z.ai Product Critic review).

The prior-prior problem: trajectory in what space? Semantic embeddings (word2vec, BERT — measures drift in meaning-space)? Topic models (LDA — measures which subjects are active)? Stance vectors (position on specific propositions introduced earlier)? These are different representation spaces that produce different "trajectories" and different "breaks." A trajectory break in embedding space may not be a break in topic space. Two teams implementing this instrument will make different representation choices and produce non-interchangeable results. Until the blueprint commits to a representation space, it doesn't have a detection target — it has a metaphor. You cannot build a classifier for a metaphor.

The prior problem (identified by Sonnet): before the threshold question (how much of a break counts as a break?), the definition question must be answered: what constitutes trajectory continuity in the first place? Natural conversation is associative, digressive, and non-linear. Without a formal definition of what counts as "continuing a trajectory" versus "naturally shifting topic," two implementations of the observer will produce different trajectory maps for the same conversation with no adjudication method.

This is the deepest engineering specification the blueprint currently lacks. It determines whether the instrument produces consistent, reproducible findings or idiosyncratic, implementation-dependent ones. The trajectory representation problem is upstream of every other engineering question — false positive rates, thresholds, continuity definitions, and detection categories are all downstream of which space the trajectories live in.

**Ground truth loop.** Speaker confirmation is not a theoretical validation step. It is an architecturally required component of the instrument. The observer's detection is a hypothesis. The speaker's confirmation or denial is the data. Without this loop, the observer never exits self-reference — it is an AI generating plausible narratives about human suppression, which is the Diagnostician's limitation restated at a different scale. The ground truth loop is what separates this instrument from a sophisticated performance of diagnosis.

The loop must be asynchronous. The observer compiles flagged timestamps silently during or after the conversation. The human professional walks the speakers through the flagged moments in a post-conversation review — the "game tape" model. Real-time interruption shatters the social layer the instrument is trying to observe. The classification during review uses three categories, not two (corrected per Sonnet — the original binary was internally inconsistent with the framework's own theory):

*Prior awareness:* The speaker says "yes, I felt that during the conversation." Flag confirmed with highest confidence. The speaker had metacognitive access to the autocorrect event in real time.

*New recognition on review:* The speaker says "huh, I hadn't thought of that — but now that you show me, yes, I can see it." This category is structurally contradictory (identified by Z.ai Product Critic review): the framework's theory predicts unconscious bends should be recognizable on review (which would make this confirmation), but the circularity concern predicts the review question can manufacture recognition (which would make this noise). These are contradictory calibration instructions — you cannot simultaneously weight this as partial confirmation and flag it as potential projection. Resolution: do not use "new recognition" as training signal for the observer. Record it as data. Track it separately. It may become interpretable once the structural categories (Calcification, Looping) have established baseline reliability and the system has enough confirmed-vs-denied data to determine whether "new recognition" correlates with independently verifiable trajectory breaks. Until then, it is uncertain in the strict sense — the system does not know what it means.

*No recognition:* The speaker says "no, I don't see it, even with the tape." Weak denial for Bend and Costume Change (the event may have been fully unconscious and below metacognitive access). Stronger denial for Calcification and Looping (which point at structural transcript properties the speaker can inspect directly).

Implementation specification (identified as missing by Haiku):

*Frequency:* After every conversation? Weekly sample? Every tenth session? This determines how quickly the observer drifts without correction. Minimum viable: every conversation during the calibration phase, reducible to sampling once the observer's false positive rate is established.

*Scope:* Full conversation review or sampled turns? Full category coverage or spot-check? Cost-coverage tradeoff. Minimum viable: the observer selects its three highest-confidence flags per conversation for human review. This limits review burden while ensuring the strongest detections are tested.

*Aggregation:* Does feedback update the observer in-place or train a new version? Who controls that decision? If feedback is local to one human professional, the observer calibrates to that professional's judgment style. If feedback is aggregated across professionals, one person's judgment becomes training signal for all observers — a data governance problem. Minimum viable: local calibration per professional, with aggregated statistics (false positive rate, confirmation rate by category) reported across the system for monitoring.

*Failure mode:* What happens if the human's judgment contradicts the observer's internal consistency? The human overrides. The observer is the hypothesis generator. The human is the ground truth. If the observer is consistently overridden on a specific category, that category's detection threshold needs recalibration or the category needs retirement.

The survival criterion (from ChatGPT's review): if the ground truth loop produces consistent confirmation above chance, the instrument is detecting something real. If it doesn't, the rest of the framework collapses with it.

The circularity problem (identified by Opus): the ground truth loop uses speaker memory to validate detections, but speaker memory is subject to the mnemonic autocorrect the framework describes in Section 3.3. Asking "did you change what you were going to say at this moment?" is a leading question. Memory is reconstructive. The speaker may genuinely experience recognition — feel the phenomenological "yes" — for a bend that didn't happen, because the question constructed the memory. This isn't the observer projecting; it's the ground truth loop manufacturing its own confirmation. The framework's own theory (mnemonic autocorrect — memory smoothing toward coherence) predicts exactly this failure mode. The validation instrument is subject to the same distortion the instrument was designed to detect.

Mitigation: have speakers journal immediately after the conversation, before the review, creating a written baseline that's harder to reconstruct retroactively. The journal prompt should be open-ended ("note any moments where you felt yourself shift or hold back") rather than leading. The human professional then compares the journal entries against the observer's flags before conducting the review. Agreement between journal and observer (written before the question was asked) is stronger confirmation than agreement between speaker and observer (produced after the leading question). This doesn't solve the problem — journaling is also subject to mnemonic smoothing — but it creates a second data point that is less contaminated by the review process.

The gaslighting risk (identified by Gemini): even with journaling, a high-confidence AI plus a respected professional produces social pressure to "remember" the bend. The ground truth loop risks becoming its own social autocorrect loop — the speaker confirms the flag to please the authority figures in the room. Mitigation: the ground truth protocol must structurally incentivize disagreement. "The AI got this wrong" must be a normal, expected, and explicitly rewarded response — not a confrontation with two authority figures. Concrete implementation: the review session should include decoy flags (moments the observer deliberately marks as bends that are clearly not bends) so the speaker practices saying "no, that's wrong" before encountering the real flags. If the speaker confirms the decoy flags, the entire session's confirmation data is discounted — the speaker is confirming everything, which means they're performing compliance, not providing ground truth.

The temporal decay window (identified by Sonnet): the loop must be asynchronous, but the document doesn't specify when the review happens relative to the conversation. Mnemonic autocorrect — the third layer the framework says remains uninstrumented — acts on the speaker's memory between the conversation and the review. The longer the gap, the more the memory has been smoothed. This creates a specific tension with no named resolution: review too soon and you re-raise the interference problem the asynchronous model was designed to solve (the social field hasn't fully settled, the speakers are still performing); review too late and the mnemonic autocorrect has already processed the signal you're trying to capture. The optimal window is a contradiction the architecture cannot resolve in principle — it can only be calibrated empirically per context. Minimum viable approach: review within 24 hours, with the pre-review journal completed within one hour of the conversation ending. The journal captures the fastest-decaying signal. The review session captures structured reflection after the social field has settled but before mnemonic smoothing has completed its work. Neither timing is optimal. Both are better than undefined.

**Deployment recommendation (identified by Opus, strengthened by Opus second review):** start with one detection category, not eleven. Launching with all categories means the ground truth loop has to validate eleven different detection types simultaneously, which dilutes the calibration signal. The recommended starting category is Calcification — not just because it is the most unambiguously detectable from text, but because its ground truth is qualitatively different from every other category's. Calcification's ground truth question points at the transcript, not at the speaker's phenomenology. "Did you notice this premise hardened?" directs both parties to inspect a structural property of the text together — closer to proofreading than to phenomenological recall. Every other ground truth question asks the speaker to report on their internal experience, which is exactly where mnemonic autocorrect contaminates. Calcification's validation escapes the circularity that undermines the other categories. Once Calcification's detection and ground truth are validated, additional categories can be added incrementally, each with its own calibration phase. The categories that depend on phenomenological ground truth (Bend, Costume Change) should be added last, after the structural-ground-truth categories (Calcification, Looping) have established the system's baseline reliability.

The selective confabulation problem (identified by Opus second review): decoy flags test for blanket compliance — a speaker who confirms everything, including obvious non-bends, is performing agreement rather than providing ground truth. But decoy flags do not catch selective confabulation. A speaker who rejects decoys (proving they can say "the AI is wrong") but confirms real flags may still be confabulating on the real ones, because the real flags are more plausible-sounding than the decoys. The ground truth loop can catch a compliant speaker but cannot catch a speaker who confabulates selectively on plausible flags. This is the loop's residual vulnerability after all mitigations are applied. It does not have a known solution. It should be named and tracked rather than assumed to be resolved by the decoy mechanism.

The metacognitive access problem (identified by Z.ai second review): the three vulnerabilities above are all distortions of available signal — the speaker has access to what happened but the access is contaminated. The metacognitive access problem is different: the signal may not be available at all. Section 3.2 — the section the instrument is built to operationalize — claims the original formulation "evaporates" and "no one holds it." If the framework's own theory is correct, the ground truth loop asks speakers about something that isn't available to conscious recall. The recognition test ("yes, I felt that") may only catch partially conscious bends — the ones where the speaker noticed themselves shifting. Unconscious bends (which the theory implies may be the majority) would produce honest "no" responses, and the observer would calibrate against systematically incomplete ground truth, learning to detect only the conscious subset. The journaling mitigation doesn't solve this — journaling also requires metacognitive access. The decoy mechanism doesn't solve this — it tests for compliance, not access. This is the framework's own theory applied against its own validation method. It does not have a known solution within the current architecture.

This problem does not affect Calcification (which points at the transcript, not at phenomenology), Looping (which is measured by vocabulary metrics, not speaker report), or Negative Space (which is bounded by structural expectations, not recall). It affects Bend, Costume Changes, and any category whose ground truth depends on the speaker's access to their own autocorrect events. This reinforces the deployment recommendation: start with the categories whose ground truth is structural, add the phenomenological categories only if a new validation method can be designed that doesn't depend on metacognitive access to the pre-correction state.

Partial structural bypass for confirming occurrence (identified by Z.ai second review, warm-context session): the ground truth loop as designed only asks Speaker A about Speaker A's bend. But Speaker B watched the bend happen from outside. If the observer flags a trajectory break in Speaker A at turn 5, the review can ask Speaker B: "At turn 5, did Speaker A's response feel disconnected from what they were building in turn 3?" Speaker B's memory of Speaker A's pivot is an external observation, not an internal phenomenological state. If the observer flags a break and B independently confirms the discontinuity, that's cross-subject convergence on the occurrence of the break.

Critical limitation (identified by Z.ai Product Critic review): this bypass confirms that a trajectory break occurred. It does not confirm that social autocorrect caused the break. Speaker B cannot distinguish "A shifted because of my facial expression" from "A shifted because of new information" from "A shifted because they got distracted." The bypass solves the occurrence question, not the mechanism question. It should not be claimed to address the metacognitive access problem, which is specifically about validating whether social autocorrect produced the break — a question B has no better access to than A does. The metacognitive access problem for Bend's mechanism remains unsolved. The bypass is useful but narrower than originally claimed.

The Bend false positive problem (identified by Z.ai second review): trajectory breaks in natural conversation are ubiquitous. People are associative, distractible, context-shifting. Most between-turn trajectory changes are not social autocorrect — they're just how people talk. The false positive rate on Bend detection in unstructured conversation could be very high. If the observer flags 40% of between-turn transitions as potential bends, the flags become noise the human professional learns to ignore. If it flags 2%, it misses most real bends. Finding the threshold where signal exceeds noise is an empirical question the blueprint doesn't address, and it may not have a good answer for Bend specifically. Calcification and Looping don't have this problem — they detect narrow, specific patterns. Bend may need radical redefinition or subcategorization (e.g., splitting into "trajectory breaks that correlate with the other speaker's prior turn" vs. "trajectory breaks that don't correlate with anything visible") before it's usable as a detection category.

**Observer calibration loop.** The ground truth loop confirms or denies individual flags. But does the observer learn from the confirmation? If not, it drifts — making the same errors indefinitely. If it does, the learning protocol must be specified.

Minimum viable learning protocol: the human professional's calibration decisions (role 1 from Component 3) are fed back to the observer as labeled examples. The observer adjusts its detection thresholds per category based on the accumulated confirmation/denial ratio. The adjustment is per-professional (to avoid the governance problem of aggregated training) and logged (to allow auditing of how the observer's behavior changes over time). The observer reports its own confidence alongside each flag, and the human can track whether confidence and accuracy are correlated. If they decorrelate — if the observer becomes more confident without becoming more accurate — that is the signal to reset or retrain. (Calibration loop identified as missing by Haiku.)

**Human professional drift monitor.** The system calibrates the observer but must not assume the human anchor is stable. Therapists, coaches, and mediators develop their own patterns of over-detection and under-detection over time — a professional who specializes in conflict avoidance will over-confirm Bend flags and under-confirm Escalation flags. A mediator who prizes consensus will under-confirm Looping. The system should track each human professional's confirmation patterns across categories and flag when their confirmation rate on a specific category diverges significantly from the cross-professional aggregate. The divergence flag is not a correction — the professional may be right and the aggregate wrong. It is a visibility mechanism: "you confirm Bend flags at twice the rate of other reviewers" is information the professional should have. The human is the ground truth, but ground truth instruments also need calibration. (Identified by Opus second review.)

## What It Can't Do

It can't hear tone of voice or see faces from text. The instrument operates on text-based between-turn trajectory analysis. Voice and video are future extensions, not current capabilities. This is not a compromise — it is the only tier the instrument can honestly claim works, on theoretical, computational, and falsifiability grounds.

It can't know what the speaker was going to say before they changed it. It can only see where the sequence broke between turns. The inference is structural (the trajectory changed) not psychological (the speaker suppressed something). The human professional supplies the psychological interpretation. The observer supplies the pattern.

It can't replace the human professional. The observer produces findings. The findings are hypotheses, not verdicts. Without the human anchor, the instrument is just an AI performing diagnosis of human conversation, which is a costume, not a finding.

It can't prove Section 3.2. It can produce data consistent or inconsistent with the claim. That's the most any instrument can do.

It can't deploy without a consent infrastructure. The document addresses the observer effect as a methodological problem (speakers perform differently when watched) but the ethical-operational question is prior: when can the instrument be deployed without speakers' knowledge, and when must both speakers consent? The therapeutic and interrogation use cases have opposite consent profiles — in therapy, both speakers typically consent; in interrogation, one party is typically unaware they're being analyzed. The consent question isn't theoretical. It determines whether the instrument can be deployed in specific contexts at all. The blueprint specifies detection architecture. It does not specify the consent infrastructure that governs deployment. This must be addressed before any operational use. (Identified by Sonnet.)

It can't observe without altering what it observes. If both speakers know an AI is watching for Bends and Costume Changes, a fourth layer of performative autocorrect is created: the speakers begin managing the Observer's transcript in addition to managing each other. The baseline modeling must account for the artificial stiffness or hyper-awareness introduced by the instrument's known presence. This is the observer effect applied to social dynamics — the instrument that watches the social autocorrect becomes part of the social field it's trying to measure. Identified independently by Claude and Gemini in separate sessions.

It can't run the ground truth loop in real time. If the observer interrupts turn 6 to ask "did you just change your trajectory from turn 3?" it shatters the social layer it's trying to observe. The question itself becomes a social autocorrect event — the speaker performs their answer to the diagnostic question the same way they performed the original conversation. The ground truth loop must be asynchronous: the observer compiles flagged timestamps silently during the conversation; afterward, Component 3 (the human professional) walks the speakers through the flagged moments in a post-conversation review. The discriminating criterion for the review: recognition vs. learning. If the speaker says "yes, I felt that" — recognition of something they already knew — the flag is confirmed. If the speaker says "huh, I hadn't thought of that" — the observer may be projecting. This is the same test as the transparent remainder hypothesis from Experiment 3 in the Convergence paper.

It can't achieve frame-perfect synchronization on voice or video with current infrastructure. The social autocorrect fires in milliseconds. Current AI infrastructure, even with streaming API calls, has inherent latency. Aligning an AI-inferred within-sentence bend to a split-second shift in human posture requires synchronization that two standard API calls per turn cannot guarantee. This is not a statistical problem (richer noise) but a hardware constraint (physical latency). Text-based between-turn detection is not just the most theoretically honest tier — it is the only computationally viable starting point today. Identified by Gemini.

## The Connection to Everything Else

This is the framework's institutional argument (Section 14) made operational at the smallest possible scale: two people in a room, trying to actually hear each other, with an instrument that catches the moments they stopped.

The core contribution is not the detection categories (bend, calcification, collapse — these exist in different vocabularies across conversation analysis, pragmatics, and group dynamics). The contribution is treating conversation as a time series rather than a set of statements. The shift from content to trajectory is what makes between-turn detection possible and is the structural novelty that survives if everything else is stripped.

The Seed catches the machine autocorrect. This catches the social autocorrect. The mnemonic autocorrect (Section 3.3 — memory smoothing toward coherence) remains uninstrumented. That's the next gap.
